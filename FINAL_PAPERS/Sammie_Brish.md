## Abstract
For this paper, I will be explaining my idea for an open-source local events website. It would aggregate, clean, and display data about community events for people to see them in one place. The project is technically focused, involving data intake and storage, as well as some UX design. Beyond that, it's really about visibility, access, and community coordination. Many community events, generally, are posted across different platforms, e.g., different social media platforms, email notifications, or different websites. That kind of separation leads to smaller events and organizations without the resources to promote their events as widely getting a lot less exposure and traction. My goal for this project is to explore the way that adding a technical structure to something like events within a local community can help to support community participation and uplift different corners of the community. 

## Purpose
The purpose of this project is to organize information to improve community access, keeping a human touch to everything. Instead of treating community events as just data, the platform would enable and empower communities, treating the events as what they are: social landmarks that shape how people experience and make memories in their neighborhoods. When information on these events is difficult to access, participation becomes limited to people who are already embedded in certain networks. This system would lower the barrier to discovering opportunities to connect with one's community, by centralizing information and presenting it in a format that is consistent, is readable, and is easily accessed. Being open-source and using widely accessible building blocks, my hope is that all kinds of communities would take the system and make it their own, adapting it to their community needs. 
Vision
My long-term vision for this project would be to create a sustainable, community-oriented platform that would support the discovery of local events while remaining adaptable to change. The goal is to keep things very intentional in terms of scope, and keep the platform modular. Local relevance is the priority here rather than capturing a wider scope of events, as well as accuracy, clarity, and ease of contribution. 
Three Guiding Principles
### 1: Accessibility Over Complexity
My first guiding principle for this project is prioritizing accessibility over technical complexity. This applies both to the users of the platform and to any contributors working on the codebase. From the perspective of the users, accessibility would mean presenting events information clearly, and taking care to not add unnecessary features that might obscure the more important basic details of events (location, time, etc). From the perspective of a contributor, accessibility means making sure architecture is understandable and well-documented, so that any future collaborators can meaningfully engage with the project. An overly complex system can appear to be "better", but runs the risk of discouraging participation in the community project and can increase long-term maintenance costs. By working with Python and Firebase, tools that are widely accessible and have strong support options, onboarding and future improvements stay easy, but future growth of the project is still supported.
### 2: Workflow Transparency
My second guiding principle is maintaining transparency in all workflows. Keeping workflows as explicit as possible helps improve communication across the project, and documenting all processes in detail, not just in development, but also in data handling and management keeps things easy to understand how all changes were made and decided on, as well as where automation would be appropriate vs. where human judgement is important. Using Github issues, labels, and a Kanban-style board would allow all contributors to see what work is planned, in progress, or completed. This kind of visibility would support accountability without creating a hierarchy, keeping things as a community effort instead of relying on authority, which is important for this kind of open project. 
### 3: Sustainable Automation
My third guiding principle for this project is using automation as selectively and sustainably as possible. Automation, when used well, can help to reduce repetitive labor and support consistency across the platform. However, when applied without the right judgement, automation can replace processes where a human touch is very important, getting rid of important nuance, which is extremely necessary for this kind of project. Automation should be used for things like scheduled data refreshes, basic validation, and issue labelling. Decisions about things like data inclusion, categorization, or community standards/norms would need to stay human-driven. Excessive automation runs the risk discouraging community participation, which is the entire point. 
Architecture and Technical Stack
The technical architecture of my project is intentionally straightforward. Python would serve as the main language for data intake and transformation. Python scripts would collect event information from different sources, clean and standardize it, and prepare it for storage. Firebase would function as the backend, and would handle things like authentication and storage. Continuous improvement and development is supported this way, and there would be enough structure to manage the data consistently. Using Firebase allows the frontend to update dynamically as new events and features are added, supporting incremental development. Features can be or adjusted without having to uproot the entire system, keeping things iterative. Github would serve as the central space for coordination, keeping version control, issue tracking, and documentation all in the same ecosystem. This makes sure that all code, process, and communication is interconnected. Issues and pull requests as well provide a visible record of decision making and changes over time. 

## Workflows
The workflow for my project follows Agile principles. Work starts in a planning phase, where issues are defined and categorized using labels such as "todo", "in progress", etc. All goals for the project, this way, become actionable tasks. Once work begins, tasks would have to go through development and review phases. Anything that gets completed will not be treated as final, but instead would feed into a retrospective stage where decisions are evaluated. This step of reflection is crucial for sustainability. This helps contributors and developers identify technical debt and adjust scope and workflows. Automation would play a limited role here. For example, automated checks might flag things like improperly formatted data or failed uploads, but decisions on prioritization and rescoping would stay human. 

## Community and Governance
Being open-source ensures that who can contribute, how decisions are made, how conflicts are resolved, and how responsibilities are distributed is shaped by the community. The core contributors would likely be a small group of community members who would be responsible for setting contribution guidelines, reviewing pull requests, and managing releases. Beyond that, other community members could submit event resources, improve features, or report errors through Github. Governance is intentionally light. Instead of any kind of formal hierarchy, norms would be clearly laid out and be shaped by the specific community and be documented. Expectations this way, are visible, without needing constant moderation. Governance, here, is embedded in every process throughout the platform itself instead of being enforced through authority. Another very important part of governance is feedback. Issues and discussion threads will provide structured spaces for any kind of disagreement or iteration. By keeping the conversations public and archived, the project will have a deep memory bank, allowing any future contributors to understand how and why any and all decisions were made. This transparency supports accountability.
Automation
Automation will play a supportive but limited role in my project, used sparingly and carefully. Automation can reduce the workload of contributors when it comes to routine, repetitive tasks, freeing them up to work on other aspects of the project where human influence is integral. For example, scheduled scripts can refresh event data regularly, and automated checks can flag missing fields of events information. This kind of automation is beneficial to maintain consistency and reliability within the platform, without needing constant human oversight. In a community-oriented project like this, though, overreliance on automation can obscure context by doing things like automatically closing issues, enforcing strict rules on contribution, or prioritizing work solely on metrics, discouraging contributors from participating and reducing trust in the platform. It's important that the platform exists to serve the community, and has low barriers to contribution, working with the community and not against it. Excessive automation can also mask underlying problems like outdated data sources, so it is important that the system is continuously evaluated. Because of this, automation should be limited only to assistive functions. Human contributors would be responsible for interpretation, prioritization, and especially for community interaction. Any automation would not be involved with governance, just supporting the workflows of the project without replacing human judgement.

## Roadmap
The roadmap that I envision for the project is geared towards a more gradual type of expansion instead of rapid scaling. In the short term, the focus would be on establishing a reliable core pipeline by identifying trusted data sources, ensuring formatting is consistent, and that events are presented very clearly on the frontend. There would be early milestones to reach like basic search functionality, category filtering capabilities, and documentation robust enough to onboard new contributors as the project grows. In the medium term, additional features could be explored, like community-submitted events or localization for different neighborhoods. Each of these additions would need the existing workflows and structures to be revisited. For example, allowing community submissions would introduce questions of verification and accountability. These challenges should not just be passed off to automation to take care of. A better option here would be documented review processes in combination with limited automated checks to flag more obvious issues. Over the long-term, sustainability depends on managing change. Data sources may shift and community needs may change over time. To accommodate this, parts of the system may have to be reworked or even replaced entirely. The roadmap, then, would include review points where contributors could reevaluate whether or not the existing practices and capabilities of the platform still serve the community's needs. These checkpoints would function as a safeguard against unchecked technical debt or misaligned priorities. They also would provide chances for collective decision making, ensuring that any changes are negotiated instead of imposed on users. Long-term sustainability also requires acknowledging that there are limitations to volunteer labor, like uneven contribution patterns, periods of low activity, and interest changing over time. By keeping things modular and well-documented, progress in the project doesn't rely on any one contributor. 
## Failure Modes
One potential risk in my project is overreliance on automation to manage data quality or behavior of contributors. Automated checks would be great for checking things like formatting errors and missing fields, but they are incapable of evaluating important community aspects like relevance, cultural contexts, or community impact. Automation cannot replace human judgement, as this would degrade trust in the platform and likely reduce participation over time, with the community likely rejecting it. Another risk is scope creep. Because local events can vary in format or structure, there is constant pressure to add special features or custom fields. Without clear boundaries, the system could become increasingly difficult to maintain. This risk is addressed through tying any kind of feature expansion to documented use cases, and requiring that any new additions justify their long-term maintenance cost. Another risk in my project, just from the nature of something like this, is invisibility. No matter how well-designed a tool is, if it is not accepted by the community it is designed to serve, it will be effectively useless. It's imperative that the platform is integrated into existing community practices, being established by the community and for it. 
## Evaluation
Evaluating the success of a community-oriented project like this requires moving past technical performance metrics. Focusing on things like data refresh frequency, page load speed, etc, would not capture whether or not the platform is actually serving its purpose to the community. The goal of the project is to improve visibility and access to local events, so evaluation should focus on community use, trust, and participation. One dimension of this would focus on accessibility and usability. This could include assessing how easily users are able to find relevant events, how clearly and completely the information presented is, and how often users return to the platform. Feedback could be collected on this through surveys to reveal if certain events are still underrepresented. This kind of qualitative evaluation is essential for understanding whether the platform is lowering barriers to participation as intended or recreating existing visibility issues. Another important dimension is contributor engagement. Since this is an open-source project, the sustainability of it depends on if community members feel empowered to contribute. Metrics like the number of unique contributors, the frequency of issue submissions, and responsiveness to pull requests would give insights into the contributor ecosystem, if these are interpreted with caution. A smaller group of consistent contributors could be more valuable here than a huge number reflecting shallow engagement. Evaluating contributor experience requires paying the necessary attention to clarity of documentation, ease of onboarding, and the tone of interactions in the discussion spaces of the platform. Impact assessment should also account for how well the platform is integrating into existing community practices. Success for this project doesn't have to look like widespread adoption, but meaningful use in communities. Maybe local organizations rely on the platform to regularly share events, or community members routinely reference it as their trusted source of information on what's happening near them. These outcomes are more difficult to measure from a quantitative perspective, but their important for evaluating alignment between the project's design and the real needs of the community. For my project, I want evaluation to be not a final step, but an ongoing process in pursuit of improvement. Keeping check-ins regular creates opportunities to reassess and make corrections. In a way, evaluation becomes a feedback mechanism for backend contributors that supports continuous improvement instead of just making a judgement as to the success or failure of the system. By framing it around community impact instead of technical optimization, we reinforce the idea that success is not centered around efficiency or scale, but by whether or not the platform actually helps community members discover opportunities to connect with their communities.
## Conclusion
By grounding my project in the principles of accessibility, workflow transparency, and sustainability, the design is meant to prioritize community needs over efficiency. The technical choices I've made, those being the usage of Python scripts and Firebase infrastructure, are intentionally modest, putting ease of contribution and onboarding above all. My project focuses on alignment between community needs and values, and tools and processes needed to accommodate them, abandoning any kind of novelty in technical complexity. My project is not one finished product, but instead a template for an evolving framework that would be shaped by the respective communities it would serve. Automation is used with caution, documentation is a central piece of the equation, and workflows are meant to be explicit to support reflection and continuous improvement and adaptation. These choices reflect the idea that a community project succeeds not because of the impressiveness of building it, but because of how it organizes and connects people, knowledge, and changes over time.
