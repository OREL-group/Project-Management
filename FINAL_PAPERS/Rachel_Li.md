## Introduction
Career preparation has become an increasingly complex and stressful process for college students and early career professionals. Beyond academic performance, students are expected to develop polished resumes, write tailored cover letters, excel in interviews, and build professional networks simultaneously and without consistent guidance. While many universities provide career services, access to personalized support is limited. Students without strong informal professional networks or prior exposure to professional norms are often at a disadvantage (Jobscan, 2025).

At the same time, the job market itself is constantly changing. In addition to the diminishing job market, students are more commonly pursuing interdisciplinary roles, multiple industries, or nontraditional trajectories. This is partially due to harsh traditional hiring practices and shifting values so people are starting to embrace nonlinear career paths where collaboration and creativity thrives (Ryder, 2025). 

With this change in job-seeker needs, the current career preparation resources are not sufficient for helping people achieve their career goals. Particularly for students just starting out on their career building journey, they have to rely on informal advice or costly third-party services to prepare resumes, locate internships, and practice for interviews.

This paper presents Northstar as a community-centered project shaped by the principles of open-source community building, documentation, and responsible management of technical debt. Additionally, it will examine the project’s motivation, target audience, design process, workflow, and long-term vision through project management frameworks.

## Project Statement
Job-seeking presents significant challenges for individuals at all career stages, but these difficulties are especially prevalent among undergraduate and graduate students and recent graduates. Limited access to consistent guidance, professional networks, and affordable career resources often makes the process overwhelming and inequitable. Therefore, Northstar is created as an open-source career preparation platform designed to directly address these challenges through shared tools, peer knowledge, and community governance that can adapt to an ever-changing job market. 

Inspired by the North Star’s historical role as a constant point of reference for navigation, the platform aims to provide orientation and reliability without prescribing a single career path. Instead, Northstar empowers users to navigate their own professional journeys through shared resume and cover letter templates, interview preparation tools, and peer-contributed insights from students, alumni, and industry professionals. Building on the principles of openness, accessibility, and collaboration, all resources on the platform are free to use, openly maintained, and continuously improved by the community. By reducing the reliance on expensive private career services, Northstar reframes career preparation as a shared public good rather than an exclusive service.

## Motivation
The motivation for creating Northstar is deeply rooted in personal experience with the challenges of career preparation. Building my own resume and navigating the job search process was much more difficult than expected because I didn’t feel like I had the support or guidance needed. I relied on resume templates found through Google with little understanding of how resumes differ across specific roles and industries. Additionally, many templates required paid subscriptions, which was frustrating. 

When seeking feedback, the experience was equally as challenging. I have gone to various people for resume reviews which resulted in different and sometimes conflicting advice– most likely because they are in different career fields. However, this made it difficult to understand which feedback was most relevant, why certain changes need to be made, and how to align my experience with a specific career goal.

There was a clear gap between what students are expected to do and the guidance available to help them succeed. College is already demanding, and adding career preparation on top of coursework without adequate support increases stress. Because of this, the idea of Northstar emerged from the desire for a free, centralized platform that explains not only what to include in career materials, but why it matters and how it connects to specific goals. By bringing resumes, cover letters, interview preparation, and peer feedback into one platform, Northstar reduces confusion and makes career preparation more navigable.

## Target Audience
Northstar is designed to support multiple interconnected user groups, starting with the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (UIUC) community and expanding outward over time. The platform intentionally adopts a phased approach, allowing it to address the immediate needs of a well defined user base while still remaining scalable to broader populations. By first focusing on UIUC students and alumni, Northstar can leverage shared institutional contexts, existing networks, and localized career pathways to establish trust, support, relevance, and early adoption. As the platform matures, its open-source and community driven structure will allow it to expand beyond UIUC, supporting a wider range of students, job seekers, mentors, and employers. This gradual expansion ensures that Northstar is able to respond to user needs while maintaining quality, reliability, and collaboration– values central to its mission.

### Undergraduate & Graduate UIUC Students
The primary target audience is undergraduate and graduate students– especially juniors or seniors preparing for internships or full-time roles. This demographic often faces intense pressure to secure employment while still completing their academic requirements. Northstar can be particularly helpful for students pursuing non-linear or interdisciplinary career paths, as well as those without strong informal professional networks. These students may lack access to insider knowledge about hiring processes, resume expectations, or interview norms, making open and peer supported resources especially valuable.

### UIUC Alumni & Early Career Professionals
A secondary audience includes UIUC alumni and recent graduates. Alumni can provide lived experiences, mentorship, and up to date insights into hiring practices that formal career services might fail to capture. Early career professionals can also benefit from continued access to shared resources while also gaining the opportunity to give back through mentorship and peer review. This reciprocal relationship strengthens the platform's community and ensures resources remain relevant.

### Faculty, Advisors, and Campus Organizations
In later stages of expansion, Northstar can be integrated into advising, coursework, and student programming through faculty, career service staff, and campus organizations. This could be a beneficial resource for advisors to stay up to date on current trends and expectations to share with students. Additionally, professional organizations on campus can use this tool to support one another regardless of previous experience or major.

### Long-Term: Employers and Industry Partners
In the long term, potentially after Northstar has achieved a sustainable following, collabing with employers with existing UIUC connections could be beneficial for both the platform and employers. Northstar’s feedback feature relies on community volunteers, but this is not sustainable because in the case where there are more review requests than reviews, it could negatively impact quality control. Creating a partnership with employers could contribute meaningful insight on real-world hiring needs, ensure there are enough contributors, and help employers understand the current hiring expectations.

## Design Process
The design of Northstar was guided by the principles of openness, modularity, and scalability. The project began with the creation of a public GitHub repository– establishing transparency and encouraging collaboration. The initial focus of this version was a bit less complex, prioritizing the resume and cover letter preparation and peer review features. This aspect of job seeking is considered the biggest barrier for many job seekers since many struggle to even land an interview (Miller, 2025). Therefore, this aspect was crucial to perfect and best suited for open contribution and iteration. Publicly available templates were then added into folders in the repository and sorted based on industry, role type, and experience level. 

For this prototype, usability metrics like how quickly users could find relevant templates and how long it takes them to use or download the materials were prioritized. This helped ensure that the platform addressed real user needs rather than theoretical ones. To do this, an index was created to link templates and streamline navigation. Additional folders were also added to support peer review, allowing users to submit anonymized materials and receive feedback directly within the repository. Throughout the design process, survey feedback was used to refine features and guide future iterations. This feedback driven approach reflects the project management principles I learned that emphasizes the alignment between user needs, project goals, and available resources.

## Principle 1: Open-Source Community Building
In this section, I will explain how the class topic of open-source community building was taken into consideration for the design of this project. Utilizing lecture 7 and the article by Matthias Stürmer, Northstar most closely aligns with a user community model. The platform exists to solve a shared problem of unequal access to career preparation. Users including students, alumni, and mentors can actively contribute resources, feedback, and knowledge to the platform. Additionally, costs are shared through contribution rather than financial transactions. Instead of paying for premium services, users invest time, experience, or feedback which reinforces the idea that career preparation can function as a common good rather than a commodity. Overall, Northstar is designed by and for its users, which is the defining feature of a user community (Stürmer, 2013). This structure supports sustainability, relevance, and trust while reducing dependence on centralized gatekeepers.
	
Taking this principle into consideration, features such as peer review were added to ensure that this platform maintains the community aspect. In addition, being free to access and use ensures that Northstar is considered open-source. Therefore, the monetary costs will be covered by sponsorships from companies and institutions interested in this platform for institutionalized use.

## Principle 2: Documentation
Documentation plays a crucial role in making Northstar scalable and inclusive. Clear contribution guidelines, template standards, and review protocols must be provided and outlined to ensure that users are able to participate without prior insider knowledge. This also ensures that the platform complies with open-source standards and minimizes privacy risks. 

Documentation also supports transparency in decision-making. Utilizing lecture 14 for effective documentation practices, I made sure to record design choices, review criteria, and workflow processes to reduce ambiguity and prevent informal power structures from dominating the platform. This principle is extremely important in creating and maintaining a platform like Northstar as it helps the creator stay organized, which in turn will also improve the quality of the platform. Overall, it ensures that all aspects in the platform, from design to use, aligns with the project’s values.

## Principle 3: Technical Debt
Managing technical debt was an essential consideration for Northstar’s long-term stability. While designing this platform, lecture 10 provided a lot of insight about what aspects to consider and implement to reduce technical debt. As the platform grows, shortcuts that can be taken during early development– such as minimal automation or manual review processes, can accumulate and threaten stability. Addressing technical debt requires proactive planning by creating things like automated anonymization tools, standard contribution templates, and structured review systems. While much work still needs to be done to continue reducing this debt, acknowledging these technical debts early on allows Northstar to avoid the common pitfall of sacrificing long-term stability for short-term progress when it is actually released.

## Reflection
While Northstar’s model offers a transformative approach to career development, its transition from a conceptual framework to a scalable platform introduces significant operational barriers. These challenges are centered on user equilibrium (# of review requests to # of reviewers), qualitative integrity, and data security which require more proactive architectural strategy to ensure long-term viability. These considerations are also especially crucial to creating a successful open-source platform in general because it utilizes complex structures like community and abiding by legal standards to function.

The most immediate challenge that needs to be addressed is the asymmetry between demand and supply. As the job market changes, the volume of job seekers requesting feedback and the number of industry mentors available to provide feedback shifts as well. This imbalance has the potential to create bottlenecks that lead to delayed response times and user complaints. As mentioned earlier, open-source platforms essentially rely on community participation to function so it is imperative that we address this quickly. Therefore, one way to mitigate this is to look beyond purely volunteers to provide feedback. Implementing a reputation system where mentors can earn professional endorsements or badges can act as an incentive to constantly participate and contribute. Furthermore, a hybrid model that incorporates contracted contributors or partnerships might be necessary to provide a baseline of guaranteed contributors to ensure all user needs are met. 
 
Another challenge Northstar faces is maintaining high-quality constructive feedback, which is also difficult to manage when relying on a workforce of volunteers. Therefore, the platform must implement standardized rubrics and peer-review mechanisms for mentors themselves. To ensure that all feedback remains ethical and free from bias, automation can be leveraged to flag non-constructive language or evaluate mentor credentials. This way, Northstar can maintain a standard of excellence and effectiveness for its user base since it is important for an open-source platform to maintain a strong user following.

Lastly, ensuring data privacy is a technical debt that must be tackled before public release. The efficacy of a resume review often relies on specific details such as company names and project titles that also happen to be the most identifiable details. While the current anonymization efforts rely heavily on user compliance, future iterations must utilize some sort of personally identifiable information detection system. This issue is tricky because on one end, over-anonymization can strip a resume of the context needed to provide mentors with the information needed to provide industry specific advice. On the other hand, under-anonymization can put users at risk or make them feel hesitant to utilize the platform because of the privacy risks. There needs to be a balance for the amount of anonymization so one suggestion is developing a context preserving anonymization method where sensitive data is masked or generalized using AI preprocessing methods.

## Conclusion
Northstar represents a fundamental shift in career preparation that utilizes the foundations of openness, community, and shared responsibility. By applying principles of open-source community building, documentation, and technical debt management, the platform addresses both the immediate challenges faced by current job seekers and the long-term sustainability of professional mentorship. 

Northstar functions as a reliable point of orientation in an increasingly uncertain job market. Its ultimate success is measured not just by the volume of materials processed, but by the strength of the community it fosters and the human-centric values it embodies. In a digitalized world of algorithmic gatekeeping, Northstar represents the values of transparency, community, and equal access to all.
