## Background
Transparency has been shown to improve trust, perceived fairness, and understanding between organizations and users. However, real estate markets remain notably opaque. Renters often lack reliable information on historical prices, neighborhood trends, and building conditions. Differences in experience between buyers and sellers, variation in building quality, and non-investment motivations for housing decisions further complicate the market. Although real estate transparency has improved globally through digital platforms and open data initiatives, access to spatially detailed and historically consistent rental price data remains limited  (Cellmer & Trojanek). 
Chicago exemplifies these challenges. I was specifically interested in Chicago because it is my ideal place to live in after graduating. Additionally, it is the city that many people I know, particularly at UIUC, call home. Chicago is one of the largest cities in the nation and a vibrant bastion of culture, industries, and art. Rising rents, declining supplies of lower-cost units, and uneven access to housing data have intensified affordability pressures across the city, and with these struggles mean that the very people who ensouled the city are also being priced out of it. While these dynamics provide the context for Chicago Rental Search, the focus of this project is not solely on diagnosing the housing crisis, but on proposing and managing a hypothetical open-source solution that addresses information gaps through transparency, community participation, and shared governance.
Research from the Institute for Housing Studies at DePaul University has shown that the city is losing lower-cost rental units faster than demand is growing among low-income renters. Competition for remaining affordable units has increased, intensifying rent burdens and displacement pressures. Particularly concerning is the decline of two- to four-unit buildings, which have historically formed the backbone of Chicago’s unsubsidized affordable housing supply. While large multifamily developments have expanded, these units are far less likely to be affordable to low-income households. 

## Project Motivation
Chicago Rental Search is an open-source project designed to increase transparency and equity in Chicago’s rental housing market. The platform would enable renters, landlords, researchers, and policymakers to access standardized, spatially organized rental data, including price ranges, unit characteristics, and community-sourced feedback. The central motivation is to reduce information asymmetry and limit the unchecked extraction of economic rent by making rental market data more accessible and interpretable. 
This project is grounded in working open principles: openness by default, participatory design, accountability through transparency, and continuous iteration informed by community input. By operating as an open-source platform, Chicago Rental Search would allow contributors to inspect, improve, and reuse both the codebase and the underlying data structures. This openness ensures that the platform serves public interest rather than private profit.
There are some existing online resources that use free, publicly available data from Chicago’s open data portal. I was able to find an interactive map and a tabular database (pictured below), respectively, but both were very limited in their usefulness because they only cover rental housing developments that are specifically sponsored by the City of Chicago programs to maintain affordability in local neighborhoods.

In other words, the data that the general public currently has access to does not include every City-assisted affordable housing unit that may be available for rent nor does it cover the (much more common) affordable housing units throughout the city that do not have City subsidies. Still, they are a good start for the project to work with to create our first build, but with the end goal of having as comprehensive data on all rental properties in the city, that then could later be filtered for by price, area, etc.  Chicago Rental Search is motivated by the belief that better data alone will not solve affordability challenges, but that transparency is a necessary precondition for more equitable decision-making by renters, landlords, and policymakers alike. Housing is a necessity, not a luxury, and Chicago Rental Search would empower users from all backgrounds and means to increase their own bargaining power.

## Vision Statement
The project would be developed and managed as an open-source initiative from its inception. All code, documentation, and non-sensitive data schemas would be publicly available under a permissive open-source license. This approach encourages collaboration, reuse, and accountability, while reducing barriers to entry for contributors.
Working open principles would guide both technical and organizational decisions. Development roadmaps, feature discussions, and design rationales would be documented in public repositories. Community members (including renters and small-scale landlords) would be invited to participate in discussions, report issues, and propose enhancements. Transparency in governance would help build trust and legitimacy, particularly in a domain where mistrust of institutions is common.
Open data practices would be balanced with ethical considerations. While aggregated price ranges and anonymized survey results would be publicly available, personally identifiable information and sensitive tenant data would be protected and anonymized. This balance reflects a commitment to openness without compromising privacy or legal responsibilities.

## Timeline Objectives
The research and planning phase will focus on identifying gaps in the existing rental housing information landscape. Surveys will be conducted to determine what renters and landlords value most in a housing search platform. Data collection will draw from public sources, surveys, and input from experienced housing researchers. A full-time domain expert will be onboarded to guide feature prioritization and ensure methodological rigor. Collaboration with a UIUX designer will support early interface concepts. The team will define project parameters, assess budget constraints, and determine which user needs can be feasibly addressed in the initial release. 
Special attention will be paid to geographic definitions. While Chicago has 77 officially recognized community areas, neighborhood boundaries are less formal. A 2024 University of Chicago study on neighborhood naming conventions will inform how neighborhoods are represented in the database and map. Similar design to this map created, users will be able to hover their cursor, or merely tap while using the app, to see the name of the neighborhood and reported price range from the lowest at the cheapest apartment/housing options to the most expensive within that area (using latitude and longitude)
During early design stages, the core team (e.g. software engineers, programmers, UI/UX designers, and domain experts) will be assembled. The first prototype of the website and app will be developed using defined user personas. Core features will include search functionality, filtering results, interactive mapping, and basic trend visualization. Internal testing with focus groups will inform iterative design improvements, performance optimization, and quality assurance.
Following refinement, the product will be released to an initial user base. The database and map will be updated at least twice a year to maintain relevance. As storage constraints emerge, historical data may be aggregated into annual averages to preserve long-term trends without excessive storage costs. Ongoing scaling will depend on sustained partnerships with leasing companies and community organizations to support data collection and verification. Existing advocacy organizations such as Chicago Housing Initiative (CHI) and Chicago Housing Justice Coalition (CHJC) could be potential collaborators who have existing community ties and stakes (specifically for affordability in lower-income households) and have likely already collected some valuable feedback. Though, notably, the resources included on CHI’s site are specific resources like vouchers for housing, tenant rights, and shelters/immediate care. Similarly, CHJC has helplines for tenants in need of legal representation. These are connected to Chicago Rental Search’s ethos towards affordability, but not directly in our purview. As for scaling, we could also potentially work alongside city and public agencies such as the Chicago Housing Authority or Chicago Department of Housing (the former of which I used as a key source in this research). Importantly, they likely have access to grants. As for domain experts to conduct surveys, analyze data collected, and design the platform itself, our project can collaborate with universities nearby for experienced researchers. Chicago is home to University of Chicago, Northwestern University, DePaul University, University of Illinois Chicago, and more institutions that all have urban planning or related social science and design programs.
The research and planning phase is foundational to ensuring the platform’s credibility and usability. Data collection will involve both quantitative and qualitative methods, including surveys, interviews, and snowball sampling through community networks. This approach allows the project to capture lived experiences while mitigating the limitations of purely administrative data. The minimum viable product (MVP) will prioritize essential features such as property-level data, neighborhood-level price ranges, and an interactive map. 
Testing will focus on usability, data accuracy, and performance under real-world conditions. Engaging community members, leasing companies, and housing advocates will be critical for data validation and long-term sustainability. Partnerships will help ensure that the platform reflects on-the-ground realities and adapts to changing market conditions.

## Project Scope
The initial scope of Chicago Rental Search focuses on delivering a functional and informative minimum viable product (MVP). The platform would be built using publicly available city data on rental prices, unit sizes, zoning classifications, and building characteristics. This quantitative foundation would be supplemented by qualitative data collected through surveys and interviews with tenants and landlords.
Key information fields would include:
 - Property address
 - Property type
 - Management company or owner category
 - Area code and neighborhood name
 - Number of stories and total units
 - Number of units currently available
 - Reported rental price range
 - Latitude and longitude
   
Neighborhood definitions would be informed by a 2024 University of Chicago study examining how residents self-identify neighborhood boundaries, acknowledging that official community area boundaries do not always align with lived experience. A central feature of the platform would be an interactive map of Chicago. Users could hover or tap on neighborhoods to view reported price ranges, housing density, and availability. Over time, the map would allow users to analyze trends and observe how affordability changes spatially.
A defining aspect of the project is its reliance on community participation. In addition to public datasets, Chicago Rental Search would incorporate qualitative surveys from tenants and landlords. These surveys would capture experiences related to rent changes, building conditions, management practices, and length of stay. To ensure relevance, tenant responses would be archived or removed five years after tenancy ends. Additionally, snowball sampling would be used to reach community members who might otherwise be excluded from formal data collection, particularly in underrepresented neighborhoods. This approach aligns with working open principles by valuing lived experience alongside institutional data.

While recognizing the limitations of self-reported data, transparency around data sources, gaps, and uncertainty would be emphasized. Users would be able to see how data was collected and where coverage is limited.

## Challenges
Several challenges may hinder implementation. Rental data is often not readily available due to confidentiality agreements and legal constraints. Missing data and sampling bias may limit representativeness, particularly in under-resourced neighborhoods. Rising labor and maintenance costs pose financial risks, while technical debt and obsolescence threaten long-term viability. Combatting these issues will require careful planning, regular updates, and sustainable funding strategies. Perhaps most consequentially, we are working with large amounts of dynamic renter data where the biggest appeal of our product is contingent on the relevance of data collection. So, the platform may degrade with poor maintenance and lack of technical innovation or public engagement. 

## Conclusion
Chicago Rental Search is a product that strives towards transparency and equity in one of the city’s most critical markets. Future features may include tenant ratings from the qualitative survey results, reviews, and reported lengths of stay. Maintaining accurate, up-to-date data and strong community partnerships will be essential. A core tenet of the project is to maintain scrupulous standards of recordkeeping, verifying prices to the best of one’s ability (e.g. cross referencing with listed online prices). Ultimately, improved data on affordability trends can help city leaders prioritize investments, protect existing affordable housing, and design targeted strategies to address Chicago’s ongoing rental housing market.
