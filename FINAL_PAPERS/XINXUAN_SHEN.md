# FlowMeet: A Collaborative Platform for Real-Time Meeting Visualization
By Xinxuan Shen, IS 340
## Introduction
In modern collaborative works, meetings are the major ways of communicating with colleagues or groupmates, yet they often suffer from lack of efficiency, information overload, and loss of shared understanding. As the conversations go longer, important ideas, decisions, and action items are often scattered, making it difficult for participants to keep up during meetings or recall key outcomes afterward. These situations often lead to unproductive meetings, which are meetings that lack clear objectives and have inadequate follow-up and often result in financial impact (“The Hidden Cost of Unproductive Meetings”, n.d.).
Traditional meeting tools such as recordings, transcripts or written notes can capture content, but users still need to organize the information on their own, and these methods lack the ability to support real-time information summaries. Recent tools such as AI meeting assistants can automatically generate a summary of the meeting content immediately after the online meeting ends, listing out the key points. They can also integrate with other project management platforms to automate the scheduling and summarizing process (Mitsuyami, 2024). Although this approach significantly improves the user's experience of reviewing meeting content, the problem of information overload during the meeting remains unsolved.
This project proposes an idea of open-source platform called FlowMeet that tracks the content of users’ meetings and generates real-time flowcharts that summarize the discussions and list key points, decisions, and action items. Rather than focusing on technical implementation, this paper emphasizes how the project can be guided, managed, and sustained.
## Background
During my first year of college, I often found it difficult to stay focused throughout lectures since I often failed to understand instructors’ words. This challenge was not solely due to language barriers but more caused by the excessive contents in 1.5 hours to 2 hours long lecture.
Unlike online lectures which allow students to review recordings, in-person classes rely heavily on notetaking. However, I often lost track of where the instructor was. Once a transition between topics was missed, it became more difficult to reconstruct the logical flow of the content afterward. As a result, both my notes and my memory often fail to include a coherent understanding of the lecture as a whole.
In a visualization class, the instructor proposed a solution that was helpful to me. Each time a new topic was introduced, the instructor updated a tree map on the slides, showing how the new concept related to previous content. This visualization of course structure made it significantly easier for me to follow the lecture, take notes, and understand the instructor’s words.
This enlightens me how powerful visualization summary can be in supporting comprehension during discussion. It also inspired the core idea of FlowMeet.

## Project Vision Statement
This project is to develop an open-source, community-driven platform called FlowMeet that supports collective sensemaking in meetings by transforming spoken discussions into real-time visualizations such as flowcharts. FlowMeet aims to help participants better understand ongoing discussions, stay aligned on key ideas, and review meeting content beyond recordings or notes. Users can also review, adjust, and interpret visual summaries based on their own judgment and understanding. The modifications on the flowcharts will be updated to all participants of the meeting.
By adopting an open-source development model, the project encourages transparency, shared ownership, and continuous improvement and sustainment through community participation. The long-term vision is to establish a sustainable community in which designers, developers, and users collaboratively shape how the platform functions.
## Target Users
The primary target users of FlowMeet consist of students and working professionals who frequently participate in online meetings or discussions, including college students, employees, project managers, team leaders, and members of collaborative teams. In both academic and professional settings, discussions often involve complex ideas, decision-making processes, and multiple participants, making it challenging to maintain shared understanding and take notes of key points during the meetings.
For students, especially those engaged in course group projects or research teams, important information discussed during meetings can be difficult to organize and review in the middle of the meetings. FlowMeet helps students visualize discussion structures, track responsibilities, and better understand how ideas evolve during the discussion. This support is particularly valuable for students who are learning to efficiently and actively participate in a meeting or struggle with notetaking during fast-paced discussions.
For professionals, meetings often involve strategic planning, problem-solving, and coordination across teams, where discussions can easily become unfocused or deviate from their original goals during long meetings. FlowMeet assists in maintaining alignment during discussions by generating real-time flowcharts that summarize key points, decisions, and action items. By continuously reflecting the structure of the conversation, the flowcharts allow participants to monitor the direction of the meeting at any moment, identify emerging priorities, and adjust the focus of discussion when necessary. Additionally, users who attend meetings remotely or join discussions asynchronously can benefit from the visual summaries, allowing them to quickly understand the context and progress of conversations without relying on notes or recordings.
## Discussions and Documentation
In the development of FlowMeet, documentation serves as a critical strategy for communication, coordination, and community building. Documentation is treated as an integral part of the project’s development process, ensuring that decisions, discussions, and design rationales are consistently recorded and shared among team members.
The project will use dedicated discussion platforms such as GitHub Discussions as a feedback board along with Slack or Discord servers to organize conversations, document decision-making processes, and foster a collaborative social network among contributors.
GitHub Discussions serves as a space for project-related conversations, including feature proposals, design questions, and feedback after major updates. This platform allows discussions to be searchable and organized by categories, which helps preserve project context over time. By documenting questions, responses, and feedback in a public place, the project makes sure decisions are transparent, which align with open-source model. This approach also lowers the barrier for new contributors, who can review past discussions to understand the project’s direction.
Slack or Discord serves as spaces for more immediate and informal communications, such as greetings, sharing of new ideas, and discussions about new technologies. These platforms can maintain social connections within the community, helping with quick coordination and relationship-building among contributors. They are particularly effective for encouraging participation and creating a sense of belonging within the project’s social network. 
While GitHub Discussions, Slack and Discord play important roles in supporting project communication, they serve different purposes and have various advantages. GitHub Discussions provides a more structured space with systematic message management and classification. Its searchable and persistent records make it easier to track past discussions, revisit decisions, and reference design rationales over time. As a result, GitHub Discussions is more suitable for professional, project-based conversations. In contrast, Slack and Discord support group chats and private messaging, which are useful in building social network. Although channels offer a basic level of organization, tracking historical records can be troublesome. This makes these platforms less effective for formal documentation but well-suited for informal discussions and idea sharing. 
By combining these platforms, FlowMeet leverages the strengths of both structured and informal communication spaces while mitigating their respective limitations through intentional documentation practices.
## Version Control and Change Management
Version control means to “control, organize, and track different versions in history of computer files” (“Version Control”, 2025) and it plays a critical role in managing change and supporting coordination in the development of FlowMeet. In the open-source project, multiple contributors work on shared code and project materials, and a structured version control system is necessary to track evolution, prevent conflicts, and support review. FlowMeet uses GitHub as its primary version control platform to support these goals.
All contributors update the project through GitHub’s pull and push mechanisms. Each push request will be reviewed by other contributors to make sure the codes run well without conflicts and bugs. Using GitHub also makes sure the development of FlowMeet is transparent and traceable. From a project management perspective, this approach supports accountability by clearly documenting who made specific changes and when those changes occurred.
Besides version control, community also includes guidelines within the GitHub repository to standardize code style and formatting. These guidelines help ensure all contributors from various backgrounds can understand the codes and participate in code development and review.
## Community Building
Community building consists of two parts, community involvement and community automation. To maintain a healthy and inclusive community environment, FlowMeet establishes clear community rules and participation guidelines. These guidelines define acceptable behavior, communication norms, and contribution expectations, helping prevent irrelevant or negative contents that could distract the project or disrupt. By setting clear rules, FlowMeet creates a respectful space for all contributors.
Communication is another key to community involvement. Updating regularly to the community and centralized information such as a README file or “Start Here” guide keep contributors informed about the project’s direction and important decisions. These practices help build trust, credibility, and strong relationships within the community.
To encourage participation, FlowMeet recognizes and reward contributors by measuring their contributions. For example, the project may highlight or reward the most active or impactful contributors monthly. These rewards are not intended to create competition, but to acknowledge effort and motivate engagement.
FlowMeet uses automation in community building to reduce repetitive and time-consuming tasks. Automation such as AI assistants can help with basic code review and format checks, allowing contributors and maintainers to focus on higher-level decision-making tasks. Automation can also support check-ins for new members and responding to frequently asked questions. FlowMeet treats automation as a tool for support rather than control, ensuring that human collaboration remains critical to the project’s growth.
## Project Roadmap
The development of FlowMeet would follow the principle of agile project management, which means delivering a project through iterative, feedback-based approach to break the project into manageable phases (Association for Project Management, n.d.; Drumond, n.d.). The development of FlowMeet is divided into 4 phases.
The first phase starts with user research and prototyping. Surveys and interviews will be conducted to identify common pain points target users have and explore expectations for real-time meeting support tools. Based on the feedback, prototypes of FlowMeet will be designed. To test feasibility with convenience, the test will rely on simulations. For example, developers can assign a person to manually draw flowchart during a meeting to mimic FlowMeet’s functionality. This approach allows the project team to evaluate whether visualizing discussion structure improves meeting experience before starting technical implement.
The second phase begins after initial user research and prototyping, with a focus on basic code implementation. At this stage, developers will prioritize core functionality, such as capturing meeting content, summarizing discussions, and generating simple flowcharts. Following the development is user experience evaluation aiming to identify usability issues, functional gaps, and confusion points. Feedback collected during this stage would be documented for future improvements.
The third phase focuses on issue-fixing and feature upgrading. Issues discovered in the previous phase needs to be solved first, including code bugs and usability problems. After that, FlowMeet’s functionality will be expanded by introducing additional visualizations beyond flowcharts and enabling users to edit or interact with visualizations. These enhancements aim to increase flexibility and usability of FlowMeet.
The fourth phase focuses on platform maturation. After FlowMeet has demonstrated effectiveness in online meeting settings, it can be adopted for in-person meetings. New technical considerations such as different interaction dynamics and input methods will be documented and addressed.
Beyond these phases, FlowMeet will keep ongoing maintenance and iterative updates, incorporating user feedback. Throughout all phases, development will be guided by principles of Agile project management. This approach allows the project to remain supportive of user needs.
## Risk Management
As a long-term project, FlowMeet faces several risks that might harm its sustainability. Two major risks are technical debt and community friction, both can impact project progress.
Technical debt is the accumulated costs caused by bad practice, miscommunication, and decision prioritizing speed rather than quality (Mucci, n.d.). Since FlowMeet is developed in multiple phases over a long period, unresolved issues or design compromises from earlier stages may accumulate and increase the workload of later phases. This risk is further amplified by evolving AI-related technologies. To mitigate technical debt, FlowMeet establishes clear standards for communication and code formatting. Shared conventions ensure that contributors can understand and build upon existing work, reducing confusion and rework caused by inconsistency. In addition, GitHub dashboards and project tracking tools are used to monitor quality-speed trade-off. By making this balance visible, the team can make decisions to prevent technical debt.
Another significant risk is community friction, which can arise from insufficient engagement or ineffective communication among contributors. When expectations are unclear or communication is poorly conducted, contributors may be assigned to roles that do not align with their skills or interests. This misalignment can lead to frustration, reduced motivation, and ultimately disengagement from the project. FlowMeet addresses this risk using guidance tree, which provides contributors with a clear overview of available roles. Guidance tree helps contributors decide where they can contribute most effectively. This structure supports better role matching, improves coordination, and reduces interpersonal friction within the community.
## Conclusion
Overall, this paper illustrates how open-source project management practices can be applied to FlowMeet. With successful project management, FlowMeet can represent an opportunity to shape the development of collaborative tools and enhance users’ experience with meetings. While FlowMeet begins as a conceptual proposal, its long-term potential lies in its effectiveness and convenience in helping with online or in-person discussions, which are critical parts of students’ or professionals’ daily work. Through ongoing refinement and engagement, FlowMeet can evolve into a platform that not only supports more effective meetings, but also demonstrates how structured management strategies contribute to sustainable and human-centered system design.
