## The Way
Samantha Mean, Spring 2026


### Introduction   

As a Christian, I have developed habits and practices that draw me closer to God and have become an integral part of my life. Some things I do daily, like pray, read scripture, and reflect on God’s movement in my life, while other times, I go to scheduled events, like life group (small group bible study), college nights, worship nights, Sunday service, volunteer opportunities, and other things. At these special events, it’s easy to mark if and when I could witness God’s work in my life, but j, my practices as a Christian are so ingrained in my life that sometimes, I lose sight of the true progress I’m making, and sometimes, that keeps me from making progress in my relationship with Christ, which is what we’re called to do.

The lack of remembrance of what the Lord has done is probably the number one inhibitor of my spiritual awareness. Even for things in general, I hear, see, or remember in the moment, but eventually forget them. This keeps my progress stagnant and caught in the same cycles and patterns of spiritual practice and relationship with God as I always do. Rather than waiting for a sudden wake-up call to reform my ways and repursue God with a new perspective, but shortly after falling into another cycle, what would it look like if there were a way to visually track and see just how much God is working in my life, and therefore move forward every day in remembrance of that compound? That is why I’m building The Way.

For people who desire to see God in their lives, I envision the development of The Way to be an app that gives users the ability to track their experiences with God to strengthen their faith.

#### Objectives
The Way is a portal for individuals seeking God to be the keeper of their spiritual rhythms and to be a tool that is conducive to remembering how God is constantly moving in their lives. The Way eliminates the spontaneity and erratic nature of our memory and grounds the user’s faith in real moments of God’s hand in their life that they recorded themselves. The Way isn’t adding to or taking away from one’s faith, but is a tool that allows the user to see their own faith and relationship with God for themselves.

The Way is a resource for remembrance, rhythm, and organization. Its design is intended to address the niche needs of people seeking God and communities that are trying to grow together and independently.

#### Vision Statement & Roadmap
The Way is an app that helps Christians draw nearer to God by providing meaningful ways to track His faithfulness, record their spiritual practices, and bring them to remembrance of who God is to them.

The development of The Way will follow an agile methodology with sprints being designed primarily for the UX & frontend development team and the data & backend development team. There are two sprints: a design sprint and a build sprint. After the two sprints, a beta of The Way will be tested and launched, then monitored and maintained to eventually become an official version of the app. I projected the app development to come to completion or at least hit every prong of the plan in six to nine months.

There are six team member types that project issues are divvied up between: Project Manager,  User Experience & Frontend Developers, Data Engineers & Backend Developers, Quality Assurance (QA) & Testing, Faith Advisor & Community, and Outreach. While some of members fall under multiple team member types, having issues and tasks be organized in this manner keeps project clarity and designation straightforward. Some team member types don’t have any specific tasks given to them during some phases of the project in Figure 1; however, I would either be providing preparation work specified on a task/issue manager like a Jira kanban board, or not recruiting that specific team member type until they are required for a certain issue or task. For instance, while the data engineers and backend developers don’t have an explicit task during the beta phase, I will be preparing them with user stories, feedback, and giving them pain points identified in the past sprint that they will still be working on even during the beta phase. And, realistically, these phases will not be completely separate from each other, especially since this project is on a short timeline. In other cases, I will be summoning a specific team member type later into the project. For example, the quality assurance and testing team members do not have a task until the beta phase. Since for at least the first round of design and build sprints, there’s nothing to test necessarily, or nothing complete enough to even be tested, they are not needed yet. Therefore, I wouldn’t start initiating any real responsibilities for the QA testers until the beta phase.

#### Community Building
Because this is a religion-based app, doing ample research and having involvement of relevant communities is so important for every step. Similar to how a medical professional or scientist is included in the development and launching of healthcare software systems, having a faith advisor to inform professionals who may not be a part of the community would be impactful for the utility of the app. Having developers and designers in the community would be preferred, since they already have a background in the experiences of the users and may have convictions about contributing to a project that would serve themselves and others that would benefit.

While I don’t see this project being an open-source project, building a culture within the whole team is fundamental for a successful implementation of agile, and makes productivity personal and beneficial to the whole team.

Before even building a community, I have to have a clear understanding of my role as project manager and scrum master. It is my responsibility to be clear on proper prioritization of issues, ensure that there is strategy alignment, to actively be planning and overseeing execution, and manage resources across projects (Bridges, 2024). In order to design a culture that is productive towards the end goal of the project, being the key holder to the whole project vision is necessary and can only be achieved by continuously communicating with stakeholders and framing it in a way that all members of the team can understand and get behind.

After establishing a unified culture around the work of the project, defining requirements as an assembled team, conducting user research with interviews and surveys, and having the faith advisor develop a framework and scope for the app’s positioning in a user’s spiritual life can be straightforward and productive.

Being a smart and clear project manager can only be effective if the team believes you. Therefore, another important factor that I must consider is being trustworthy and truly caring for the needs of not only the end users of The Way, but the team themselves as well (Dr. Alicea, 2026). It is even more important because of The Way’s implicated impacts on the lives of the users.

Making sure that the app respects the user’s religious practices, does not have an ulterior motive and impact on users, and is created transparently and with ethical governance is of utmost importance. Ensuring that there is not a shadow of a doubt that this app was built with pure intentions and clean, benevolent code is something that should be taken seriously.

I will achieve this by building rapport with each team contributor, checking Github repos, discussion forums, and mentions. Another way I can make the team vision, objectives, intent, and culture known is to have it written in an accessible and obvious location where each team member must encounter it often. Another facet of team culture that I would implement is a learning culture. Gamification in the community at an organization is proven to increase intrinsic involvement and conviction that work members are contributing to (Andreev, 2023). Because we will be working on a cross-functional team, to incentivize good communication and learning across different members with different roles, I would do team member shoutouts during retrospective meetings and give opportunities to demonstrate cross-functional collaboration for a prize of some sort. This instills fun, intrinsic motivation, and overall further aligns the work style of the team through making it like a game.


#### Developing in Agile
As mentioned, I will be leading the development of The Way within an agile framework. Because I plan for the app to be launched within 6-9 months (at least a first draft of it), the use of a spring model and agile methodology makes the most sense. Agile methodology is an approach that divides work into phases, emphasizing collaboration, continuous delivery, and adaptability ([atlassian.com](http://atlassian.com/)). With agile, prioritization of rapid execution is put first, and is designed for cross-functional teams. This is a great framework for my project plan, because the design and UX/frontend developers will be working in parallel to the backend and data developers. I chose this strategy to ensure the team sees the development of The Way as a unified effort. This also prevents having to reengineer from the ground up if there were to be any discrepancies between the two teams’ deliverables, making their work unable to integrate at the end.

Not only am I choosing agile because of the timeline and flexibility of facilitating a cross-functional team, but I’m also just a social person. Agile is designed with a high importance of human interaction, and rigidity in the process is actually discouraged. I perform best at being a liaison between people, so having agile be already in the architecture of the design and build of the app puts my team on the same page and flows with my project management style smoothly.

#### Scrum
More specifically, I want to implement a Scrum framework of agile methodology to lead my project management. Scrum theory refers to lean thinking, empiricism, and continuous improvement (Drumond, 2026). This means that experience, value, and momentum are of utmost importance. These are all necessary priorities in short-term, robust projects such as the one that I’ll be working on.

Another reason why I believe agile is the most effective framework of project management is because of its iterative nature. Because I’m having a design sprint for both design and development teams, cross-checking, reviewing, and making incremental progress and quickly adjusting to wrinkles between the teams will nip issues in the bud quickly. As will be mentioned in the following sections, the retrospective review is incorporated in the roadmap of The Way’s development.

A scrum sprint is a short, time-limited period where a team strives to complete a set amount of work (Rehkopf, 2026). I decided to compose the development of The Way into three sprints: Design, build, and beta.

##### The Design Sprint
Before the design sprint is initiated, the requirements defined by the team should be validated and approved by me, the project manager, and sent forward on the roadmap as the objectives for the next sprints. The objective for the UX and frontend team is to design the wireframes and components of the app. The backend and data engineers will be designing the database schema and backend code to suit the wireframes and components that the UX and frontend engineers will be designing. I chose to design it this way so that both segments of the team have their portions of app development developed in congruence to one another. Although I’ll be approaching development within a Scrum framework, I will also be incorporating the V-model as the software development lifecycle. My concept of having design & architecture building and frontend & backend development be parallel is drawn from and supported by the V-model (Ruparelia, 2010). On top of that, requirements defined before the design sprint will be intended to decompose with project development and be expanded upon, like shown in the V-model. Although the model is a variation of the waterfall model, not agile, it guides my project development neatly and accurately. This philosophy and sequence of execution extend to all phases and sprints of the project.

##### The Build Sprint
Unlike the design sprint, integration of build and design development will be in “real-time”, meaning cross-functional work should be happening daily throughout the sprint. The design sprint would involve more communication and cross-checking. The build sprint is for the application of Unity. The faith advisors would be checking for functionality for the objectives of the app development, and outreach would initiate as deliverables become more and more presentable.

##### The Beta Sprint
Finally, the beta sprint will likely be the fastest cycling sprint, where feedback, user stories, and issues will be produced at a higher rate, and the team will have to respond and redevelop/redesign to address every grievance brought by the QA testers. All work done during the beta phase will be to prepare The Way for launch. The Way will hit app stores, the beta will eventually get approved for launch, ministry endorsers would be sought for, and the launch campaign would begin during this phase.

In the roadmap illustrated in Figure 1, I made sure to incorporate review by the faith advisor, documentation done by me, and approval/rejection points to direct the direction of project execution. Before, during, and after sprints, I will be constantly redefining, adjusting, or developing sprint objectives to suit the positioning of where the whole team is at throughout the process. This will look slightly different during the design and build sprints compared to the beta sprint. Because the design and build sprints are the meat of the app development, goals may differ, and the cycles of progress may be longer than sprint cycles within the beta sprint. The beta sprint’s primary intent is to ramp the draft app up to launch standard, and this will require a response to beta testing with modifications to the deliverables of the design and build sprints.

Fitting to the agile methodology, I will be posing as both the scrum master, while hopefully a cofounder or another team member would be the product owner. Through the partnership of the product owner and I, the scrum master, scrum can be fully implemented and run in a way that would bring the most relevant and fitting progress to the state of the project.


### Conclusion      

I am super excited and confident in the success of my project execution strategy. Community building sets up my team and my headspace to be unified, agile methodology propels our app development into a fast, adaptable, and honest rhythm of cycles that produce good deliverables, and having a hybrid of the scrum framework and V-model software lifecycle seals cross-functional and attentive teamwork in our workflow. The Way is an app that requires integrity, trust, and passion in its developers. I plan to ensure it through the gamification of project contributions and by developing real relationships with my team. The Way requires the backend of the app to be intuitively paired with the frontend programming of the app. I plan to make that happen by developing within the framework of a hybrid of scrum and the V-model. I want The Way to be developed within 6–9 months. I plan to propel development on that timeline with the agile methodology.
